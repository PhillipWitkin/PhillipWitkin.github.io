My Fractal Art

I have been experimenting with fractal art for many years. As a digital art form, I find it fascinating, because it exploits something which would ge genuinely impossible without computational power - each pixel often requires thousands of calculations. However, at the base of these calculations are relatively simple equations, usually only a few lines long. They are not "assembled" from composing elements in the way that other digital images are, but a window upon which we can see mathematical structures which are, in a sense, discovered, but in another, already existing, awaiting a specific view of an endless and infinitely scalable structure.

3D Fractals

Fractals in the complex plan have been around since the 1970's, when there was sufficient computational power to visualize equations such as the Mandlebrot and Julia sets. Genuinely 3D fractals are somewhat newer - in 2007 the first visualizations of the "Mandelbulb", a new equation defined with a 3 vector basis, which like the complex plan fractals, originate with iterations of a simple equation set, and display self-similar behavior, but in 3 distinct dimensions, making it one of the first 'Truly 3D' fractals. They appear to define volumes much like actual objects; however, unlike Cartesian 3-space, these fractal equations utilize vector spaces which have some strange mathematical properties (beyond being fractals), so their 3D status is debateable. Regardless, they can create some interesting images.
